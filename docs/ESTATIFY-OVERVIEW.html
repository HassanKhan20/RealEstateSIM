1 — Executive summary

Estatify is a Next.js web application that gives aspiring real estate agents and investors something the industry doesn’t currently offer: realistic, no-stakes practice. The product spans three connected tracks plus a beginner mode.

  • Agent Simulations — AI roleplay against real estate personas, graded by Claude Sonnet on rapport, discovery, objection handling, and close.
  • Paper Trading — 28 synthetic Tampa listings with realistic prices, rents, taxes, and flood zones. Run live financial models across four investment strategies.
  • Beginner Mode — simplified buy-with-cash-or-finance experience plus a forward-simulation engine modeling five market scenarios.
  • Exam Prep — AI-generated, scenario-first practice questions across nine licensing topics with explanations.

All of this sits inside a single, well-organized Next.js codebase split into frontend/, backend/, and shared/ — the same separation a Node + React monorepo would have, but contained in one project.

2 — The problem and why we built this

The single most-cited statistic in real estate education: 75% of new agents quit within their first year. Among the 25% who stay, the dropout rate by year five is reported around 87%.

Why agents fail

  1. Financial unpreparedness for the 90-day commission gap. First close averages 90+ days into the career. Most have no runway.
  2. Brokerage onboarding is “here’s a desk, go sell.” Mentorship is widely promised but rarely delivered.
  3. Pre-license courses are universally described as boring — read-quiz, read-quiz format, decoupled from the actual job.
  4. Reality TV (Selling Sunset, Million Dollar Listing) skews expectations. New agents arrive expecting glamour, find rejection.
  5. Unrealistic confidence going into the first cold call. Most have never practiced a real conversation.

Why investors fail

  1. Analysis paralysis — perfect deal, never bought.
  2. Emotional buying — first deal becomes a money pit.
  3. No exit strategy before signing.
  4. Underestimating rehab + carrying costs on flips.

Estatify exists because practice closes the gap between knowledge and competence. Pilots train in flight simulators before flying real planes. Surgeons rehearse on cadavers. Sales teams roleplay. Real estate is the only major career where your first day costs $50,000 to find out if you fit.

The product is not training in the boring “course content” sense. It’s reps. Hundreds of low-stakes reps until the first real call doesn’t feel like the first call.

3 — Product features

3.1 Landing page

A cinematic, editorial marketing page with a dark hero (massive metallic “ESTATIFY” wordmark over a procedural city skyline), real photography in the carousel, a featured-properties section with a stylized neighborhood map, and editorial sections explaining the product.

First impressions are non-trivial. The dominant aesthetic in real estate is either utilitarian (Zillow) or cheesy (realtor headshots). We wanted Estatify to feel premium and serious — closer to Compass / Sotheby’s than Aceable’s animated memes.

3.2 Agent Simulations /practice/[slug]

Five fully voiced AI personas: Marcus Whitaker (motivated seller, pre-foreclosure), Linda Reyes (sharp cash-buyer investor), Dave Kowalski (anti-agent FSBO seller), Marcus Hayes (lowball investor), and Priya Chen (panicking first-time buyer).

Conversations run by Claude Haiku in-character with hidden backstory. After 3+ user turns, the user can “End call & grade” — Claude Sonnet evaluates the transcript and returns structured JSON with rapport, discovery, objection handling, close scores, plus biggest win and biggest miss feedback.

The grade feeds an ELO-style SimScore. Tiers: Rookie → Journeyman → Closer → Top Agent → Shark.

Agent burnout in year one is fundamentally a rejection-tolerance problem. The skill of staying calm when a homeowner hangs up — that’s the actual job. You can’t train it from a textbook. You train it by doing it. Estatify gives you 100+ reps before your first real call. Every persona prompt includes a Fair Housing guardrail; the grader explicitly flags violations.

3.3 Market & Deal Analyzer /market /market/[id]

Zillow-style listings grid with 28 synthetic Tampa-area properties. Filter by neighborhood, type, beds, max price. Click a listing to open the Deal Analyzer — a live financial model with four strategy tabs: Buy & Hold, House Hack, Flip, BRRRR. Recomputes in real time as you tweak down payment, rate, rehab, rent. Shows monthly cash flow, cash-on-cash, cap rate, DSCR, total cash in, plus 1% rule and 70% rule flags.

Most beginner investors fail at the acquisition stage — overpaying because they didn’t run the numbers correctly. Estatify lets you fail at this 50 times in an afternoon. Synthetic data lets us ship without a regulatory wall while keeping every number realistic. The generator is deterministic on a seed, so the same property looks identical on every reload.

3.4 Beginner Mode /beginner /beginner/[id]

Simplified version of the buying experience. Removes mortgages-vs-hard-money complexity. Two paths: all cash or finance (pick a down payment, rate, term). Plus an always-visible Simulate panel that runs your portfolio forward 1-10 years against five market scenarios:

  • Base case — uses actual property assumptions
  • Soft market — 40% appreciation, 50% rent growth
  • Hot market — 160% appreciation
  • 2008-style crash — -30% appreciation + a one-time -22% hit at month 6
  • Rates spike — +2.5% interest rate shock on financed mortgages

The full Pro Deal Analyzer assumes you know what an ARV is, that BRRRR stands for something. Most people who are real-estate-curious (the largest TAM) don’t know any of that yet. Beginner Mode is the on-ramp. The Simulate panel makes time legible — you can’t intuit 30 years of compounding from a textbook.

3.5 Portfolio Pro /portfolio

Full deal tracking dashboard for properties bought in Pro Mode. Shows total equity, monthly cash flow, average cash-on-cash, holdings with strategy badges, and a time machine — fast-forward your portfolio +1, +5, or any custom number of years. Sell at projected value with simulated 6% closing costs.

3.6 Exam Prep /exam

AI-generated practice questions across nine licensing topics: agency, contracts, property ownership, finance, math, Fair Housing, listings, escrow/title, valuation. Pick a topic and count, get scenario-first multiple choice with explanations.

First-time pass rates are dismal — California averages 45-50%, Florida 40-60%. The killers are math and state law nuance. Existing providers (PrepAgent, Aceable) are huge improvements over textbooks but still mostly flashcards. Scenario-first AI questions with explanations feel like a tutor, not a quiz.

3.7 Dashboard /dashboard

Activity hub. Current SimScore tier, daily streak, best streak, total sessions, win rate, average grade, exam average, holdings count, monthly cash flow. Plus quick actions and recent sessions list.

Daily-loop products live and die on whether the user feels their progress. Streak + tier mechanics are why Duolingo retains 5x better than the average language app. The streak isn’t the product; the streak is what gets you to come back tomorrow and run another rep.

4 — System architecture

The four-folder split

RealEstateSIM/
├── app/         # Next.js routes (must stay at root)
├── frontend/    # All client UI + client-only state
├── backend/     # Server-only logic (Anthropic SDK + secrets)
├── shared/      # Pure logic — used by both client and server
└── public/      # Static assets

This is the same separation a Node + React monorepo would have. The constraint is that Next.js requires app/ at the project root — but everything else is reorganized for clarity.

Why this structure

  • frontend/ has React + browser APIs (localStorage). Bundled to the client.
  • backend/ has the Anthropic SDK, environment secrets, prompts. Server-only — never reaches the browser.
  • shared/ has zero React, zero window, zero process.env. Pure functions and data.
  • app/ is just routing. Page files import from frontend/components/; route handlers delegate to backend/ai/.

When reading the codebase you immediately know whether a file ships to the user’s browser. backend/ and app/api/*/route.ts — no. frontend/ and any client component — yes. shared/ — both.

Why app/api/*/route.ts files are 10-25 lines

The API routes are intentionally thin. All logic lives in backend/ai/. The actual AI business logic — picking the model, constructing the system prompt, parsing structured JSON, calling ELO math — lives there. That keeps the route files boring and replaceable. Tomorrow we could swap to a separate Node server, or to Cloudflare Workers, by porting backend/ai/ and rewriting the thin handlers.

Why shared/types.ts exists

Types like BeginnerHolding are used by both frontend/lib/store.ts (which writes them to localStorage) and shared/beginnerSim.ts (which simulates them forward). If types lived in frontend/lib/store.ts, then shared/ would have a forbidden dependency on frontend/. shared/types.ts keeps the dependency graph clean.

5 — Tech stack and rationale

LayerChoiceWhy
FrameworkNext.js 14 (App Router)One project = frontend + API. RSC for fast static landing pages, client components where state matters. File-based routing means the file structure is the sitemap.
LanguageTypeScript (strict)Catches a class of bugs at compile time. End-to-end types from data → UI mean refactors don’t break things silently.
StylingTailwind CSSNo CSS files to manage. The design system (colors, fonts, shadows) lives in tailwind.config.ts. Custom helpers (btn-primary, card) live in globals.css.
AIAnthropic Claude SDKThe roleplay needs character consistency over multi-turn conversations and structured grading output. Haiku for chat (fast, cheap, in-character). Sonnet for grading + exam (better reasoning, structured JSON).
PersistencelocalStorage (v1)The MVP needed zero infra. All user state lives in the browser. Helpers in frontend/lib/store.ts. DB migration is a one-file swap.
HostingVercelEdge for static, Node runtime for /api, automatic CI/CD via GitHub. Default for Next.js.
FontsInter + Playfair DisplayInter for UI. Playfair as the display serif for editorial headlines. Subsetted and self-hosted by Next at build time.

Why no separate state library (Redux, Zustand)

localStorage + the native storage event are sufficient at this size. When two components need to react to the same change, they listen for the storage event. We’ll add a state library when we hit the limits of that pattern — not before.

Why no auth / user accounts (yet)

The product works without them. localStorage persistence per browser is fine for MVP. Auth enters the roadmap for cross-device persistence or B2B brokerage cohorts.

6 — Data flow: a graded scenario, end to end

This is the canonical example of how the four folders cooperate. Walking it once explains the whole architecture.

USER ──[1]──▶ ChatInterface (frontend/components/feature) state: messages[], scenario │ ├──[2]──▶ POST /api/chat │ │ │ └─▶ app/api/chat/route.ts (10 lines) │ │ │ └─▶ backend/ai/chat.ts → processChat() │ │ │ ├─▶ shared/scenarios.ts → getScenario(slug) │ │ (system prompt + persona backstory) │ │ │ └─▶ backend/ai/client.ts → Claude Haiku │ │◀─────[3] in-character reply ────────────────────────┘ │ ├──[turns repeat] │ USER ──[4]──▶ ChatInterface.endCall() │ └──[5]──▶ POST /api/grade │ └─▶ app/api/grade/route.ts │ └─▶ backend/ai/grade.ts → gradeTranscript() │ ├─▶ backend/ai/prompts.ts → GRADING_INSTRUCTIONS ├─▶ backend/ai/client.ts → Claude Sonnet └─▶ Parse structured JSON │ └─▶ shared/elo.ts → newRating() │ [6] Component receives response │ └──[7]──▶ frontend/lib/store.ts ├─▶ setSimScore(newRating) └─▶ addSession({ ... }) │ └─▶ window.dispatchEvent(StorageEvent) │ [8] Nav, Dashboard re-render with new SimScore

Key observation: every layer has a clear job. The route handler doesn’t know how to grade. The frontend doesn’t know what model is used. The shared math doesn’t know it’s being called from a server route. Each piece can be replaced independently.

Demo seed

frontend/lib/demoSeed.ts runs once on first page load. It populates localStorage with a realistic, lived-in state — SimScore 1285, 12-day streak, 8 graded sessions, 4 Pro holdings, 3 Beginner holdings, 6 exam attempts. New visitors immediately see the Dashboard look meaningful instead of empty.

Future migration

Replacing localStorage with a real database (Postgres, Supabase, Convex) only requires swapping helper implementations in frontend/lib/store.ts. Every consumer keeps working unchanged. Types in shared/types.ts map directly to DB tables.

8 — AI integration

Claude Haiku backend/ai/chat.ts

Used for: in-character roleplay during agent simulations. Why: Haiku is fast, cheap, and excellent at maintaining persona over multi-turn conversations. Latency matters in chat. Max tokens: 400 — keeps responses tight.

Claude Sonnet backend/ai/grade.ts & backend/ai/exam.ts

Used for: transcript grading and exam question generation. Why: Both tasks need structured JSON output and careful reasoning. Sonnet does this much better than Haiku. The grading rubric requires citing specific phrases from the transcript; the exam rubric requires legal accuracy and unambiguous distractors.

Graceful degradation

backend/ai/client.ts exports an isConfigured() check. Every route handler short-circuits to a 503 when ANTHROPIC_API_KEY is missing. Non-AI features (Market, Beginner, Portfolio, Dashboard) all work without the key.

12 — Roadmap and honest tradeoffs

What’s working

  • The four-folder architecture is clean and explainable.
  • Synthetic data generator is realistic enough that the math feels meaningful.
  • AI roleplay + grading is differentiated — no incumbent (PrepAgent, Aceable, Kaplan, MaverickRE, Hyperbound) bundles all three tracks.
  • Codebase is small enough that a new contributor can be productive in a day.

What’s risky

  • Practice as a standalone consumer SaaS is a hard sell. Research-validated: people don’t pay for “fun reps.” They pay for transactional outcomes (deal analysis, retention insurance, exam pass guarantee).
  • AI roleplay is crowded at the B2B layer. Hyperbound ($18M raised), MaverickRE, SalesGo are selling roleplay-as-a-service. Estatify’s moat has to be real-estate-specific data and bundling.
  • State-approved pre-license course pivot is a regulatory minefield. Each state is a 9-12 month approval process. Solo founder time-to-revenue is 12-18 months minimum.

Plausible paths forward

  • Path A — TikTok funnel + free product. Build an audience around “Would you buy or sell?” daily content. Monetize via brokerage cohort licenses in year 2.
  • Path B — B2B brokerage retention. Sell to brokerage operations directors at $25-50/seat/mo as “agent retention insurance.” 75% of new agents quit, each lost agent = $5-15k recruiting cost wasted.
  • Path C — Free aptitude test as top-of-funnel. “Are you built for this?” 90-second test, archetype result, shareable card. Funnels into the deeper product.

Whatever go-to-market path we choose, the engineering foundation supports it. The core product loop — agent simulations + paper trading + exam prep — is genuinely differentiated and worth building. Architecture is solid. Codebase is maintainable.
